## The real cost of unplanned downtime

In modern manufacturing, a single hour of unplanned downtime can cost tens of thousands of dollars in lost production, rework, and emergency labor. KUKA robots are built for reliability — but like any precision machinery, they require systematic maintenance to stay at peak performance over years of continuous operation.

Preventive maintenance (PM) is not just about following the manual. It is about building a disciplined program that catches wear, drift, and degradation before they cascade into critical failures.

## Core preventive maintenance intervals

KUKA defines maintenance intervals in **operating hours** rather than calendar time, because a robot running two shifts accumulates wear far faster than one running a single shift. The most common intervals are:

| Interval | Typical tasks |
|----------|--------------|
| **500 h** | Visual inspection, cable routing check, error log review |
| **1,000 h** | Gearbox oil level check, brake test, axis calibration verification |
| **5,000 h** | Gearbox oil change (all axes), battery replacement, drive fan inspection |
| **10,000 h** | Deep mechanical inspection, harmonic drive wear assessment |

Always cross-reference with the specific robot's **Operating Manual** — intervals vary by model (KR QUANTEC vs KR AGILUS vs KR CYBERTECH, for example).

## Key maintenance tasks in detail

### 1. Gearbox lubrication

The harmonic drives and planetary gearboxes in KUKA robots require the correct lubricant at the correct level. Using the wrong grease — or skipping changes — accelerates wear and can result in catastrophic gearbox failure. Replacement gearboxes are expensive and have long lead times.

### 2. Encoder battery replacement

KUKA robots use battery-backed absolute encoders to retain axis positions across power cycles. When the battery voltage drops below threshold, the robot loses its mastering data and requires re-mastering before it can run again. This is a preventable stoppage — batteries should be replaced on schedule (typically every 2–3 years depending on usage).

### 3. KRC controller maintenance

The KRC4 and KRC5 controllers have cooling fans, filters, and hard drives (or SSDs) that require periodic attention:

- **Filter mats**: clean or replace every 6–12 months depending on the shop environment. Clogged filters cause thermal shutdowns.
- **Cooling fans**: inspect for wear; replace at first sign of noise or speed reduction.
- **Drive backup**: periodically archive the KSS image to an external medium so recovery from a controller failure takes hours rather than days.

### 4. Cable and harness inspection

Robot cables flex thousands of times per day. Inspect the **dressing pack** (the cable bundle routed along the arm) at every PM for chafing, cracking, or exposed conductors. Pay particular attention to the area near the A1 axis where cable motion is greatest. Replace before failure — a cable break mid-production is far more costly than a planned replacement.

## Building a PM schedule

A practical PM schedule should:

1. Log every robot's running hours (exportable from the KRC log or an OPC-UA connection to your MES).
2. Set calendar reminders at 80% of each interval as a lead-time buffer.
3. Maintain a **critical spares kit** on site: encoder batteries, filter mats, cooling fans, and the most common fuses.
4. Document every intervention in a maintenance log, including observations and part numbers replaced.
